# Bussey & Bussey — Writing Principles

This document is the spine for any future Bussey & Bussey article, page, or written deliverable. It's drawn directly from the five-article cluster published in May 2026 (`automate-customer-service`, `business-process-automation-tools`, `business-process-automation`, `business-process-automation-software`, `workflow-automation-tools`) and from the founder's own batched material that produced them.

Two layers below:

1. **Principles** — what Bussey & Bussey actually believes about the work. This is the worldview. It does not change article to article.
2. **Mechanics** — how the principles get turned into copy. Voice, structure, the disciplines used during drafting.

If a future piece contradicts the principles, it isn't a Bussey & Bussey piece. If it follows the principles but skips the mechanics, it'll read like AI filler. Both layers matter.

---

## Layer 1 — The Principles

### Problem first, never tool first

The tool is downstream of the workflow. The workflow is downstream of the problem. We sell solutions to problems, not features. Any article that starts by listing tools — or any sales conversation that does — is doing it backwards. Owners shop tools when they should be mapping their actual work.

### "Best" only means one thing — it fits

Quality, speed, and feature counts are not what makes software best. What makes a tool best is whether it works perfectly for *this* business. Mediocre-but-fits will out-perform high-quality-but-doesn't, every time. Fit beats fancy. Stop reading feature lists.

### Map the simple path most clients are already on

The angry customer, the weird edge case, the worst-case interaction — those are exceptions you design *for*, not what you design *around*. Build the system for the path the majority of your customers are already on. Treat escalation as a feature of the system, not a failure of it.

### The tool category is sliding toward obsolete

What used to take a development team weeks now takes a small team plus AI agents a fraction of the time and cost. Connector tools (Zapier-shaped) are losing their job as native connections improve. The narrow single-purpose subscription tool is increasingly something that can be built for you directly. We're not anti-tool — but the listicle premise is collapsing, and the people selling tools can't say so.

### A pile of features is not a workflow

Most software just logs data. The floor, not the ceiling. We want software that *studies* what you put into it and brings back something solid — that analyzes the data, shows you where things can be improved, and helps your team. Storing isn't working.

### Build it once, under one roof, and own it

Most of the time, the tools a business actually needs can be built under one roof for a fraction of what they're already paying for a single subscription. Then it's theirs — license and software outright, no monthly rent, no vendor with a hand in their pocket. (We also offer rental for clients who prefer it; ownership is the differentiator.)

### Off-the-shelf is genuinely right for commodity functions

Don't out-build QuickBooks. Don't out-build a calendar app. Bookkeeping, payroll, generic scheduling, basic email — buy the proven thing. Save custom work for the parts of the business that are differentiators — the things that are different on purpose because that's how you win.

### Discipline beats shortcuts

Mapping every contingency feels tedious and ridiculous. That's exactly what makes it work. Same as fitness — wanting to be fit doesn't replace going to the gym. Building a great system takes time spent researching, failing, rebuilding, and slowly critiquing. Owners say they want this; most aren't willing to do it. That's why most don't have it.

### Pull back from the point of pain

The problem is rarely where it hurts. The supplier-fax example: missed items off the truck isn't an ops-manager problem, it's an upstream problem — the fax. Look everywhere, not just where the pain lives. The value is in the digging. And often the biggest savings show up in something completely unrelated to what brought us in.

### Keep humans in the loop where it matters

Automation done right isn't about removing every human. It's about removing tedium and placing the human exactly where their awareness and judgment matter. Pause-and-confirm points. Reference signals. Visual dashboards so a person can see what the system is doing at a glance. We do this on purpose, not as an afterthought.

### Expertise is invisible to the person who has it

The heavy-equipment operator with twenty years of unconscious technique. The home-health manager who knew chest x-rays were good for five years but never thought to mention it. Pulling that knowledge into the open is the actual work of automation. It can't be done from a brochure.

### AI is not always the answer

We love AI. We build with agents. But sometimes a simple comparison process is cheaper, faster, and more reliable than reaching for AI. The view matters more than the tool. The question is always "does this solve the problem," not "is this AI."

### Confidence is only as good as input — automation can fail silently

The screen says it's working. The numbers add up. The form looks complete. Meanwhile the actual file never landed in the folder, or the numbers were skewed from the start, or the API broke two weeks ago and nobody noticed. AI makes this worse, not better — it can be confident *and* wrong. This is why checkpoints and validation matter, and why tools without them aren't really automation; they're a screen that lies to you politely.

### "Will it replace people?" is the wrong frame

If everyone protected their job, innovation would have stalled a century ago. If a position can be replaced by AI, the position was inefficient. We focus on being *valuable*. There is always room for valuable people. Automation just raises the bar on what valuable means.

### Saving face kills growth

The fear of having picked the wrong tool is what keeps owners locked in poor systems. The risk of choosing wrong is only really a risk if you plan to *keep* the wrong thing rather than admit it. We have walked away from clients who refused to replace bad choices because they didn't want to admit they were bad. Ego is the enemy of progress.

### The norm is where good companies go to die

Owners who won't do the work settle into the norm. Trucks pushed into the ground. Buildings never painted. Systems dwindle into extinction, slowly, year by year, while the owner is too busy to look up. We have to be better than that — and so do our clients.

### We represent our clients

When our team researches tools or builds a system, the question isn't "what can we do to help." It's "how do we put this client ahead of everyone in their space." We push ourselves to be up to date on what's possible because the wrong recommendation puts a client behind. The seriousness of the person doing the work is what makes any of this work — yours, ours, anyone's.

## Layer 2 — The Mechanics

### Voice

**Harsh-but-professional.** Direct, unsentimental, confident. No hype. No flattery. We write for service-business owners, not job-seekers or consumers.

**Words we don't use.** *Amazing. Transform. Unlock. Revolutionize. Game-changer. Seamless. Effortless. Empower. Leverage* (as a verb). Anything a SaaS landing page would put on a hero section.

**Sentences we don't write.** Anything that sounds like a brochure. "In today's fast-paced business environment." "Don't just X — Y!" Any opener that congratulates the reader for caring about productivity.

**Tone we do use.** A working operator talking plainly to another working operator. Slightly weathered. Willing to say uncomfortable things. Willing to tell a story against itself. The kind of person who'd point out the wrong tool you bought even if it makes the room awkward.

### Substance comes from the founder

The whole edge is real operator expertise. AI-only filler is what Google explicitly penalizes — and what every page-one competitor already does. So the writing process is always:

1. Research first (what's the page-one landscape, what pain is genuinely out there, what's structurally true that nobody else will say).
2. Ask the founder operator-grade questions, drawn from that research.
3. Founder batches in real material — stories, opinions, calls.
4. Compile the article *around* that material, not the other way around.

A piece that's been written without founder input isn't a Bussey & Bussey piece. It's filler with our name on it.

### Stories are the centerpiece, always anonymized

Every strong article in the cluster has at least one real story:
- The self-checkout reframe (#1).
- The supplier fax and the home-health TB-test/chest-x-ray (#2).
- The White Shovel receipts and the bank-notification trick (#3).
- The I-9 forms that showed on the surface but never landed in the folder (#4).

These are what readers remember. They are also what proves operator expertise to a sharp reader. Anonymize clients (generic descriptors like "a home health agency," "an agency we built for"). No real names unless explicitly approved. No unverifiable numbers — no "$2M saved," no "47% increase." Specifics that can't be checked weaken trust.

### Word count is a guide, not a target

Be the most complete *genuinely useful* version of the answer. Padding to hit a number is the AI-filler failure mode. A tight 2,300 words of real substance beats a bloated 3,500 with 1,200 words of fluff Google penalizes. Across the cluster, articles landed at 1,090 / 1,210 / 2,340 / 2,140 / 2,880 — every one shorter than the SEO plan's "words to win" estimate, and every one defensibly complete.

### Structure

- One H1, the title. Keyword in title.
- Keyword in the first paragraph. Naturally throughout. No stuffing.
- H2/H3 sections, skimmable. Sectioned thinking, not stream-of-consciousness.
- Each section earns its place. If a section can be cut without weakening the thesis, cut it.
- Prose over bullets. Use lists only when the content is genuinely a list. Bulleted tools-and-tips formatting reads like the listicles we're competing against.

### The closing energy

Most articles in the cluster close on a hard, honest line:
- "The tools were never the point. The workflow was."
- "The norm is where good companies go to die."
- "If you're trying to save face, you can't grow. And we won't be able to help you."

Closes earn the rest of the article. They aren't soft "let us know how we can help." They state a truth and let it sit. Then a clean line about the Assessment.

### The Assessment as the natural ending

Every article in the cluster ends pointing back to what we actually do — without it feeling like a CTA. "That's exactly what we do in an Assessment." Followed by a one-or-two-sentence description of what that means in the context of *this* article. Then one final brand line. Never "click here." Never "schedule a call today." The article does the work; the close just names the next step.

### Interlinking inside the cluster

Articles reference each other where ideas overlap. Format: parenthetical inline link, never a "related articles" sidebar. Example: `(We made the same point about [keeping a human in the loop](/articles/business-process-automation/) — automate the routine, keep the person for the moments that need one.)`. This builds the topical web Google rewards, and it keeps readers reading.

### Defensibility

We never assert numbers we can't stand behind in a conversation. If the research has a specific stat, we either:
- Cite it carefully with the kind of phrasing that's true (`one benchmark found...`, `the pattern shows up in...`), or
- Convert it into a directional pattern (`can quietly cost`, `often runs into`).

We name competitors and tools factually when it serves the reader — Zapier, Make, n8n, QuickBooks, Publix, Amazon. We don't write hit pieces. We describe what each thing does, where it fits, and where it doesn't.

### Handling things we can't verify

If the founder makes a claim we can't ground (capability, specific savings, a competitor's internals), we either ask before writing or phrase it conservatively. The handoff rule: no unverifiable numbers, no overclaiming capability. A sharp prospect will hold the claim against us.

### Publishing

Articles are markdown files in `site/src/articles/`. Frontmatter matches `site/src/articles/the-work-that-shouldnt-be-manual.md`:

```
---
layout: layouts/base.njk
title: "Title with keyword"
description: "Meta description with target keyword, ~150 chars."
date: YYYY-MM-DD
draft: false
author: Bussey and Bussey
summary: "Short summary for the list view."
tags:
  - article
permalink: /articles/keyword-slug/
---
<div class="container post">

# Title

Body in markdown.

</div>
```

Permalink slug is keyword-relevant. `draft: false` so it publishes. No chat card on articles.

Pushing to `main` auto-deploys to staging. Production is separately gated. Review on staging before promoting.
